Who actually receives food stamps?

A FRESH look at the facts.

The stigma placed on recipients of food stamps — that they just don’t want to work, that they are exploiting the system — is not only misguided, it’s incorrect. More importantly, this stigma prevents or delays people from seeking help that they need. People of all backgrounds receive food stamps, but the vast majority of them were already struggling to make ends meet when they were hit with an a job loss or reduction in hours, or an unexpected expense, often due to illness or disability. We’ve taken a deep dive into our applicant data, and have gathered their stories to provide a more accurate picture of who receives food assistance in America.

Making Ends Meet

People who have never been on food stamps often think that they are for people who are somehow different from them. In fact, food stamps are a way of coping with a challenge that almost everyone is familiar with: making ends meet. The most common dilemma GetCalFresh applicants mention in their applications is not being able to afford food after paying for rent, utilities, and gas or fare to get to work.

Cost of living

Outside observers often try to place the blame on food stamps recipients for their situation. Yet, the most common challenge that our applicants face is one that Californians knows well. California’s high cost of living is a systemic problem that leaves most residents more vulnerable if they unexpectedly lose income or have an increase in expenses. A single car accident, medical emergency, or unexpected layoff can put a family over their budget for months, often forcing a choice between something as essential as food, housing, or medical care.

Between jobs, unstable work, underemployed

  • The majority of people on food stamps work (among our applicants, 57.8% of those who are ages 25 to 49 and who have stable housing are working)
  • Our applicants who are working are often looking to move from part time to full time or to a better paying job
  • Our applicants who have recently had hours cut or lost their job are looking for a better job
  • When it comes time to renew food stamps after six months, the most common reason applicants tell us for no longer needing food stamps is that they got a better job.

Many CalFresh applicants are facing unemployment. Having food costs covered helps them focus on finding new work without worrying about feeding their family. One applicant writes: "My unemployment ends June 30th. I have not yet found steady work. I am trying my hardest to find work and keep food on the table for my child. I am desperate to feed my family and I really need help."

Unexpected illness or disability

  • Applicants themselves get disabled or ill and can no longer work, but receiving SSI is a long process - often taking up to two years
  • Applicants may need to stay home to take care of sick or disabled family members, thereby reducing their income.
"My doctor told me I was disabled. I won't receive income until I am approved for disability insurance... In addition, my significant other (whom I live with and share the bills) suffered from a compound fracture in her ankle two days ago... The doctor is not allowing her to work for four to six weeks and she only has 600 dollars in her bank account."
— Applicant from San Diego County

Stigma prevents people from getting the help they need to get back on their feet

  • Stigma is a form of shame that society uses to discourage behaviors that are harmful. In part due to stigma, 3 in 10 Californians who would qualify for SNAP are not receiving it.
  • We should encourage people who need the help to apply, whether they be friends or family.

What are the myths that lead to stigma?

  • 61% of Americans above the poverty level and 41% of Americans below the poverty level believe that welfare benefits encourage people to stay poor (LA Times / AEI, 2016).
  • 36% of all Americans think that those on welfare programs would rather stay on welfare than earn their own living (LA Times / AEI, 2016).
  • People who are below the poverty level are much more likely to say that it’s very hard for the poor to find jobs, compared to those above the poverty level (71% vs. 51%, (LA Times / AEI, 2016)).
"I became pregnant unexpectedly... I have always worked and have been on my own since I was 16. I went into preterm labor 8 weeks early and was unable to work. I didn’t receive my maternity leave as expected and was put in an unfortunate situation."
— Applicant from Orange County
